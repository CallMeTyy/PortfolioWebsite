Properties of Tinkering Materials


13-02-2026

During the second lecture/lab I, together with the other two people at my table, explored four different tinkering objects. We explored the totem maker kit, the minecraft maker kit, littlebits and micro:bits. The table below shows our findings. I also made some pictures along the way, which are shown below the table :)

Totem maker kit Minecraft maker kit Littlebits Micro:bit
Affordance: Self Guiding Relatively self guiding , requires instructions to make more complex shapes. Not easy to imagine things what to do with it without instructions. Very hard to use, very large instruction book Needs a little introduction to learn what colors represent what part of the output, easy to use and learn afterwards Microbit has a website to easily start working with it, but you do need instructions to use it
Affordance: Preciousness Comes equipped with a saw to break it, it's not precious by design It's a breadboard with mostly programming related workings. The thing that does things is very precious and should not be broken. Each part is very important and at the same time can be skipped. It's not something you'd fiddle with though, you leave it the way it comes out of the package Very precious as it's just one piece, cannot really make modifications to it
Affordance: Skills needed Requires precise motor skills and tool usage. Not suitable for kids, easy to use for adults Requires a lot of programming skills, but might be easy to learn programming with if you already are motivated to play minecraft. You need very little skills to operate it, only need basic understanding of color and soundwaves Requires a little experience with programming but can also be a very good tool to learn it. Very accessible
Affordance: Interoperable Uses very standard things and can easily be connected to anything with holes Can make minecraft an input for other things as it connects to the raspberry pi Does not really work for other elements, might have some input ways that can connect with USB but did not try those. Not interoperable The output/input contact pins can make it communicate with a variety of different other devices
Novelty Very similar to many tools, not a new idea but a good execution At its time very cool and novel, now looks very dated with an old version of minecraft. Novel idea, outdated execution. Very novel idea, very interesting way to learn and play with sound waves. Very easy to use and there's nothing like it I've seen before. The simplicity is what makes it novel. Arduinos or raspis can have a similar experience, but the amount of feedback and simple sensors make it novel. Also the accessibility is new and novel.
Low threshold, High ceiling Easy to get into, gonna be very complex to make complicated things High threshold to get into, probably also a high ceiling to master. Again, easy to get into. I assume when you play with it a lot more you might be able to get some really cool sounds. Very low threshold to get into, just open a website and hook it up to a pc. With different programming languages and the potential make the ceiling high too.
Price Expensive to get into, 100 euros for the starting kit. Relatively cheap to keep going, 10-30 euros for the actual things you use. Fairly expensive, 60 euros Expensive to very expensive. Starting kit is 80 euros, full kits are up to 400 Cheap, accessible. About 16 euros

The green cells indicate the best score for a device (columns) in a given catagory (rows), red the worst score. It's overall pretty well balanced, but the minecraft maker kit is overall the worst.
