Recyclability How recyclable are the materials and components? Can parts be reused? Explain whether this is realistically recyclable or only theoretically recyclable. Note reuse potential between sessions. Link to sustainability where useful.
Durability How well does it hold up under repeated use? Describe likely wear points. Note whether software or ecosystem dependency affects durability. Explain whether it is robust in practice.
Overall Sustainability Take into account reuse, lifetime, waste, dependency, and long-term usefulness. Explain the trade-offs instead of giving a short score. Relate this to the actual tinkering context. What makes it more or less sustainable overall?
Strength Physical strength, wear resistance, fragile points, or failure modes. Describe what breaks first. Explain whether the strength fits the intended use. Keep it practical and concrete.
Storage (Size & Weight) How easy is it to store, sort, carry, and pack? Does the packaging help or make it worse? Is it shelf-friendly, workshop-friendly, classroom-friendly? Relate this to actual use.
Cost (Manufacturing) Estimate how expensive it is to make and distribute. Use cautious phrasing if you are inferring. Link manufacturing complexity to the retail price if relevant. Explain your reasoning.
Alteration & Repair Can it be cut, changed, fixed, reconfigured, or repaired? Describe how easy it is to modify after damage or during experimentation. Explain what kinds of tools or expertise repair requires. Relate this to tinkering freedom.
Availability Is it easy to buy or source again? Is it niche, outdated, school-specific, or broadly accessible? Explain whether availability changes its value as a tinkering material. Note the likely suppliers or context if useful.

These critical properties work especially well when you want to discuss sustainability and long-term design quality in context.
