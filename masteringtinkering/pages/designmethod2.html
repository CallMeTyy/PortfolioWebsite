Introduction

For this assignment I looked at problems from my own field: computer science and interaction technology, with a focus on software architecture, user research, and iterative product development. These are all areas where the problem is usually not fully clear at the start. Because of that, they are good candidates for tinkering. Instead of moving straight toward one fixed answer, a tinkering approach allows for trying, reflecting, reframing, and gradually discovering what works.

In line with the course, I approached each idea as a small tinkering playground with a seed, a toolbox, a target group, suitable support and scaffolding, and a discovery-oriented goal rather than one single correct end result. The sketches of the materials and setups will be added later.

1. User Research Toolkit

The problem. In many student projects and early-stage product teams, user research is either too abstract or too fragmented. There are many available methods such as interviews, observations, probes, surveys, diary studies, and sensor logging, but beginners often do not know when to use which method, how to combine them, or how to create a full research plan.

Why this is suitable for tinkering. User research is not a single fixed recipe. It depends on the context, the participants, the constraints, and the kind of questions you want answered. That makes it ideal for tinkering. People can try combinations of methods, rearrange them, test sequences, compare trade-offs, and discover what kind of research setup fits a specific problem best.

How it would be made. This concept would become a modular card-based toolkit. One card set would contain research goals such as understanding behaviour, measuring frustration, identifying workarounds, or comparing alternatives. Another set would contain methods, for example interviews, observations, think-aloud, diary studies, cultural probes, co-design sessions, and sensor-based logging. A third set would contain contextual factors such as time available, number of participants, privacy sensitivity, setting, and technical access. Additional cards could represent sensors and data sources, such as audio, motion, location, gaze, button presses, or physiological data. By combining cards on a board, a team could physically construct a research plan from start to finish.

The seed. A good starting seed would be a concrete prompt such as: "Design a user research setup for understanding how students get distracted while studying" or "Find out why users abandon a health app after one week." That gives enough direction to start, while still leaving room for exploration.

Toolbox and materials. The toolbox would include method cards, sensor cards, participant cards, ethics cards, timeline strips, budget markers, and a planning canvas. I would also include a few example starter packs that show how one simple research setup can be built, so that beginners can remix it instead of starting from nothing.

Target group. The main target group would be computer science and interaction technology students, junior UX researchers, and multidisciplinary design teams that need to plan research but do not yet have a lot of methodological experience.

Onboarding and scaffolding. The onboarding should begin with one example problem and one pre-built research plan. After that, participants can modify it. Scaffolding is especially important here, because beginners often struggle with choosing between too many methods. Support could be provided through prompts like "Do you want broad or deep insight?", "Do you need self-report or behavioural data?", and "What data can you realistically collect?" Ethics cards and limitations cards would also help keep the process realistic.

Suitable goal. The goal would not be "pick the correct method", but rather to design a coherent and justified research setup that fits the question. The discovery lies in seeing why certain combinations of methods give richer insight than others.

Suitable assignments. Example assignments could be: build a user research plan for a smartwatch interface, compare two different research setups for the same problem, or redesign a weak research plan so that it includes triangulation between methods and data sources.

2. Physicalisation of Code and Software Architecture

The problem. Codebases and software architectures are often difficult to understand because relationships are invisible. New developers see files, classes, services, and APIs, but not always how tightly components are coupled, where complexity accumulates, or which parts cost the most time to maintain.

Why this is suitable for tinkering. This is a strong tinkering problem because architecture is rarely fully understood by reading alone. It becomes clearer through moving pieces around, introducing changes, testing dependencies, and seeing the effects of refactoring. A physical representation makes hidden structure visible and turns abstract code relationships into something that can be explored hands-on.

How it would be made. I imagine this as a tabletop system made up of modular pieces. Each service, module, or component would be a physical block or tile. The size of the block could represent maintenance cost or implementation effort. Strings, rods, or magnetic connectors between pieces would represent dependencies. Different colours could show different concerns such as ownership, language, criticality, or volatility. Small weighted tokens could be added to represent performance cost, technical debt, bug-proneness, or API calls. The system could then be reconfigured physically to test alternative architectures.

The seed. A useful seed would be a small broken or messy architecture, for example a web app with a tightly coupled frontend and backend, duplicated logic, and one overloaded service. Participants would start by mapping it physically, then begin changing it.

Toolbox and materials. The toolbox would include component tiles, dependency links, technical-debt markers, performance-cost tokens, refactor cards, failure cards, and a board with zones such as frontend, backend, storage, and external systems. Optional digital overlays could later connect to real code metrics, but the first version should stay low-tech and tactile.

Target group. The target group would be software engineering students, junior developers, system designers, and possibly research teams discussing complex interactive systems.

Onboarding and scaffolding. Onboarding should start with a very small architecture that everyone understands, such as a to-do app or webshop. Then the facilitator can introduce one metric at a time: first dependency, then complexity, then maintenance cost. This avoids overload. Scaffolding would include example mappings, scenario cards like "traffic doubles" or "database becomes unavailable", and prompts such as "which link causes the most friction?" and "what can be separated safely?"

Suitable goal. The goal would be to discover where complexity lives and how design choices affect maintainability, resilience, and team understanding. It is not about one perfect architecture, but about making trade-offs visible and discussable.

Suitable assignments. Example assignments could be: physically map a small codebase, reduce coupling without removing functionality, redesign a monolith into clearer modules, or compare two architectures and argue which one would cost less time to maintain over a year.

3. Physical Sprint Planning Tool

The problem. Sprint planning is often treated as a flat digital list where tasks look equally sized even when they are not. In reality, some tasks are tiny, some take multiple days, some are uncertain, and some block many others. Traditional planning boards hide this physical sense of scale, which makes it harder to reason about capacity, parallel work, and risk.

Why this is suitable for tinkering. Sprint planning is a good tinkering problem because teams usually do not know the best sprint shape in advance. They need to experiment with grouping, sequencing, splitting, and re-estimating work. A physical system makes these trade-offs visible and invites collaborative planning instead of just filling columns.

How it would be made. I would design this as a board with a visible time grid for the sprint, for example ten working days. Tasks would not just be cards, but physical blocks with length representing time. A half-day task would be a short block, a two-day task a long block, and uncertain tasks could be taller blocks or include an uncertainty ring around them. Dependencies could be shown with clip-on connectors or strings, while blockers could be represented by red caps that physically prevent a block from being placed before another. Team members could each have their own lane on the board, making capacity instantly visible. This would make sprint planning feel closer to fitting real objects into limited space.

The seed. The seed could be a backlog of mixed-size tasks for a realistic product sprint, for example implementing login improvements, fixing two bugs, preparing a usability test, and deploying analytics. The team would be asked to plan a sprint with limited capacity.

Toolbox and materials. The toolbox would include time-scaled task blocks, uncertainty caps, dependency clips, priority markers, capacity lanes, and interruption/event cards such as "urgent bug", "team member unavailable", or "task turns out larger than expected". This would let teams not only plan, but also simulate what happens when a sprint changes halfway through.

Target group. This would be useful for software project teams, scrum teams, students learning agile methods, and multidisciplinary groups that need to understand capacity and task size in a more intuitive way.

Onboarding and scaffolding. The onboarding should begin with one simple rule: one unit of block length equals one unit of time. After that, the facilitator can introduce uncertainty, dependencies, and interruptions step by step. Scaffolding is important because otherwise people may treat it as a game without reflecting on why a plan is good or bad. Reflection prompts could include "where is the risk?", "which work cannot happen in parallel?", and "what should be split before the sprint starts?"

Suitable goal. The goal would be to create a sprint plan that is not only ambitious, but realistic, balanced, and robust when things change. The real discovery comes from seeing how task size, uncertainty, and dependency interact physically.

Suitable assignments. Example assignments could be: build a sprint plan for a team of four, re-plan the sprint after an interruption, split oversized tasks into smaller units, or compare a "maximally full" sprint with a more resilient sprint and reflect on the difference.

Conclusion

All three ideas take problems from computer science and interaction technology and make them more tangible, explorable, and collaborative. The user research toolkit turns research design into a combinable method playground. The code physicalisation turns hidden architecture into something visible and movable. The sprint planning tool turns time, risk, and capacity into physical properties instead of abstract labels.

What connects them is that each one supports tinkering in the sense of starting from a seed, working with a toolbox, experimenting through iterations, and discovering better questions and better structures along the way. Sketches of the kits and sample configurations will be added later.
